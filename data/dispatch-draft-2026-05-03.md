# Sovereign Dispatch: The Open Frontier Just Closed In

**Subject: Sovereign Dispatch — 2026-05-03 | The Open Frontier Just Closed In**

## This Week's Signal

### 01. The Code Won't Care Who Built It

In a live coding competition, **Kimi K2.6** — an open-weights model from Moonshot AI — finished first. GPT-5.5 came third. Claude Opus 4.7 came fifth. Gemini Pro 3.1 came sixth.

This was not a benchmark. This was real-time reasoning, coding, and execution against an objective scoring system. The top two finishers were both Chinese. The Western frontier labs filled slots three through seven.

For years the assumption held: open-weights models couldn't close the capability gap. That assumption just died in public. When the gap narrows to the point where task design determines the winner, "open-weights" stops being a compromise and becomes a strategic advantage. No API key. No rate limits. No terms of service. Anyone can download the winner.

The frontier just got crowded. And some of the new entrants brought their own source code.

### 02. The Courtroom Is Content Now

The Musk v. Altman trial — already producing leaked emails, testimony about xAI training Grok on OpenAI models, and a juror who fell asleep during testimony about charitable tax deductions — will get a live audio stream next week. YouTube. 11AM to 5PM ET. Real-time.

There's a difference between *reporting* on a trial and *broadcasting* it. Reporting curates. Broadcasting lets it flow. When the participants are Elon Musk (who can't resist a live microphone) and Sam Altman (whose company is valued at half a trillion dollars), the line between legal proceeding and reality show gets thin fast.

What we've learned so far:
- Musk confirmed under oath that xAI used OpenAI models to train Grok
- Musk told Altman to "at least change the name" when OpenAI went for-profit
- OpenAI's lawyer described the for-profit term sheet as "pretty plain vanilla"
- A juror fell asleep during testimony about charitable tax deductions

This isn't just about OpenAI's corporate structure. It's about what happens when the organisations building AGI are also the ones fighting over who owns it. The live stream turns that fight into entertainment. It gamifies the process. It makes spectators of us all while the actual stakes — who controls the most powerful technology ever built — get reduced to daily drama updates.

### 03. The Seven and the One

The Pentagon signed classified AI agreements with OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Nvidia, xAI, and Reflection. The stated goal: build an "AI-first fighting force."

Anthropic — previously a $200M Pentagon partner — was deliberately excluded. The reason? Anthropic refused to loosen "red lines" around mass domestic surveillance and fully autonomous weapons.

This is the first major friction point where an AI lab's stated safety principles directly cost it a massive government contract. Anthropic's CTO previously called the situation a "separate national security moment." Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth then called Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei an "ideological lunatic" in a Senate hearing.

So on one side: OpenAI, Google, and Nvidia taking classified military contracts. On the other: Anthropic getting frozen out for having principles, then publicly insulted by a cabinet secretary.

If the US military is indeed becoming an "AI-first fighting force," which labs get to supply the weapons? And what happens to the ones that say no?

---

## The Verdict

The open frontier just closed in from both sides. From the east: open-weights models proving they can win at the frontier. From the west: military contracts, live-streamed trials, and the steady consolidation of AI power behind gates that most will never pass through.

The signal is clear: capability is democratising. Access is not.
